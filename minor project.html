Historical Places in India

Taj Mahal

The Taj Mahal, located in Agra, is an ivory-white marble mausoleum built by Mughal Emperor Shah Jahan in memory of his wife Mumtaz Mahal.

Hampi

Hampi, a UNESCO World Heritage Site, is an ancient village in the south Indian state of Karnataka. It's dotted with numerous ruined temple complexes.

Red Fort

The Red Fort, located in Delhi, was the main residence of the Mughal emperors. It is a symbol of India's rich history and its architectural grandeur.

Qutub Minar

The Qutub Minar, also in Delhi, is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and the tallest brick minaret in the world, built in the early 13th century.

Mysore Palace

Designed by the English Architect, Henry Irwin, the Mysore Palace dominates the skyline of Mysore. A three storied structure in the Indo-Saracenic style built between 1897-1912, the palace has beautifully designed square towers at cardinal points, covered with domes.

Dwaraka

Dwarka is a city in Gujarat, India, located on the western coast of the country on the banks of the Gomati River. It's known for its rich culture and heritage, and is an important pilgrimage center for Hindus from all over the world.

Ayodhya Ram Mandir

The Ayodhya Ram Mandir, or Ram Temple, is a Hindu temple in Ayodhya, Uttar Pradesh, India. It is dedicated to Lord Rama, a central figure in Hindu mythology and the seventh avatar of Lord Vishnu. Hindus believe the temple is built on the site of Ram Janmabhoomi, the mythical birthplace of Rama, and the city of Ayodhya is considered sacred to Lord Rama's life and rule. Ajanta & Ellora Caves

The Ajanta and Ellora Caves are two collections of ancient rock-cut caves in the Aurangabad district of Maharashtra, India. They are both UNESCO World Heritage Sites and are considered to be among the finest examples of Indian art and architecture.Ellora Caves: A set of 34 caves that contain Buddhist Chaityas or halls of worship, Viharas or monasteries, and Hindu and Jain temples. The caves feature sculptures and architecture from the three religions that were prevalent in India at the time.

Humayuns's Tomb

Exemplifying the formative stage of the Mughal structural style, Humayun's Tomb stands as a landmark in the development of Mughal architecture, and also represents the earliest extant specimen of the Mughal scheme of the garden tomb, with causeways and channels.The complex encompasses the main tomb of the Emperor Humayun, which houses the graves of Empress Bega Begum, Hajji Begum, and also Dara Shikoh, great-great-grandson of Humayun and son of the later Emperor Shah Jahan, as well as numerous other subsequent Mughals, including Emperor Jahandar Shah, Farrukhsiyar.
